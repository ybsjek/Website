These are free educational materials to help customers better understand the enforcement process.

What Is a Debtor’s Examination?

Overview

A debtor’s examination is a court process used to ask the judgment debtor questions under oath about assets, income, and property that could be used to satisfy a judgment.

Why It Matters

When a creditor does not know where the money or property is, this process can help uncover information that is not visible from public records alone.

What Happens

The debtor may be required to appear in court or before an authorized official and answer questions about bank accounts, employment, property ownership, and other financial details.

How It Helps Collection

The information gathered can support later enforcement steps such as liens, wage garnishment, or a writ of execution.

What Is a Writ of Execution?

Overview

A writ of execution is a court order that allows the enforcement of a judgment by directing a levying officer or sheriff to seize property that can be used to satisfy the debt.

Why It Matters

This tool can be useful when a debtor has property or assets that can be located and taken through the legal process.

What It Can Do

Depending on the law in the jurisdiction, a writ of execution may be used to seize non-exempt property, sell it, and apply the proceeds to the judgment balance.

When It Is Used

It is often used after other collection efforts have not produced payment, or when the creditor has information about specific assets.

What Is Wage Garnishment?

Overview

Wage garnishment is a legal process that allows a creditor to collect part of a debtor’s paycheck through an employer after a judgment has been entered.

Why It Matters

It can provide a steady path toward payment when the debtor has regular employment income.

How It Works

The court usually issues an order that requires the employer to withhold a portion of wages and send those funds toward the judgment.

Important Limits

There are usually legal limits on how much can be taken, and those limits can vary depending on the type of debt and the jurisdiction.

Property Liens

Overview

A property lien is a legal claim against real property that can help secure a judgment debt. In many cases, a judgment creditor may record the judgment so it attaches to property the debtor owns, which can make it harder to sell or refinance without addressing the debt first.

Why It Matters

Liens do not automatically put money in your hands, but they can create pressure and preserve leverage while other enforcement tools are considered.

What It Can Do

Depending on the state, a lien may affect a debtor’s ability to transfer property, refinance, or sell real estate without paying the judgment first.

Practical Use

For creditors, the practical value of a lien is that it can turn an unsecured judgment into a more secured position and improve your place in the collection process.

Turnover Orders of Intellectual Property

Overview

A turnover order is a court order requiring a judgment debtor to surrender property or interests that can be used to satisfy a judgment. When the property is intellectual property, that may include copyright interests, trademarks, licensing rights, royalties, or other transferable intangible rights.

Why It Matters

These orders can be especially useful when a debtor has limited cash but owns valuable rights that still have market value.

What To Look For

Because intellectual property can be more complicated to identify and value than ordinary assets, the creditor usually needs clear records showing what the debtor owns and whether those rights can legally be transferred or collected.

Practical Use

Turnover remedies are often used when ordinary collection methods do not reach the asset. The court order should identify what must be turned over and how it will be enforced.

Subpoenas

Overview

A subpoena is a formal legal demand that can require a person or organization to produce records, appear for testimony, or both. In post-judgment collection, subpoenas are often used to gather information about income, assets, bank accounts, business interests, and other evidence that may help locate collectable property.

Why It Matters

Subpoenas are useful because they can force the disclosure of facts the debtor may not volunteer. They may also reach third parties such as employers, banks, accountants, or business partners who hold information relevant to enforcement.

What It Can Do

A subpoena can narrow the search for assets and support other enforcement actions like liens, garnishment, or turnover requests.

Practical Use

The scope and method of service matter, so the document must be prepared and served carefully to be effective.
