How The Process Works

A judgment is powerful because the court has officially ruled that one person owes another money. The person who won is called the judgment creditor, and the person who lost is called the judgment debtor.

That distinction matters because it means the debt is now backed by the court, and the law gives the judgment creditor many ways to discover assets, income, and property that may be used to satisfy the judgment.

No two judgment debtors are the same. Some have wages, some have bank accounts, and some have vehicles, real estate, or business assets. That is why every collection effort has to be built around the facts of the case.

1

Understand the judgment

The first step is to understand what the court awarded, who owes the money, and what time limits or enforcement tools may apply.

2

Identify assets and income

The court allows discovery tools that can help reveal income, property, and other assets tied to the judgment debtor.

3

Choose the right collection method

Depending on the facts, collection may involve garnishment, liens, execution, sheriff sales, or other lawful methods.
